The Ability to Do Otherwise

Is an ability to do otherwise necessary for the kind of free will that is sufficient for moral responsibility?

I argue that the ability to do otherwise is necessary for the kind of free will that is sufficient for moral responsibility. The discussion centres on two nested questions:

(A) What is the kind of free will sufficient for moral responsibility?
(B) Is an ability to do otherwise necessary for that kind of free will?

(A) presumes that moral responsibility requires free will, a widely-accepted assumption I will maintain in this essay. Henceforth, there are two kinds of free will on which moral responsibility may depend: libertarian and compatibilist. (B) then questions whether the ability to do otherwise is a necessary predicate of either kind of free will. Libertarian free will, by virtue of its incompatibility with determinism, unequivocally necessitates the ability, defined in a traditional externalist sense, to do otherwise. Correspondingly, compatibilist free will does not. However, I argue that the compatibilist conception of freedom, nevertheless, appeals to some other form of ability to do otherwise. Therefore, whether under deterministic conditions or not, I assert that some sort of ability to do otherwise remains necessary for any kind of free will that aims to support moral responsibility. Since this argument is self-evident for libertarian free will (by definition of its externalist sort of ability to do otherwise), this essay will focus on compatibilism, establishing Frankfurt's case against the Principle of Alternate Possibilities (PAP), thus showing that there exists another condition of reasons-responsiveness for freedom and moral responsibility, but contending that it is nonetheless predicated on the ability to do otherwise.

Frankfurt's Argument Against the PAP

According to Frankfurt (1969, p. 829), the PAP states: "a person is morally responsible for what he has done only if he could have done otherwise". I will challenge the PAP's notion of alternate possibilities with Frankfurt-style cases (FSCs). Frankfurt denies the PAP by presenting FSCs in which one is unable to do otherwise but still remains morally responsible:

Suppose Black wants Jones to perform a certain action, and will act to ensure that Jones will perform it. Jones, however, for reasons of his own, decides to perform this very action on his own volition, without Black having to interfere and change his mind/action. (Frankfurt 1969, pp. 835-836)

Frankfurt (1969, p. 838) asserts that Jones is not absolved of moral responsibility by his inability to do otherwise, since he acted as he did not "only because he could not have done otherwise". Given that Jones was compelled by "whatever it was that actually led [him] to do what he did" (Frankfurt, 1969, p. 837), and not by Black, he would still have made the same decisions even without Black. Frankfurt thus declares the non-necessity of the ability to do otherwise for anyone to be held morally responsible.

Frankfurt's detractors have attempted to defend the PAP by proving FSCs have alternate possibilities, but let us suppose these rebuttals fail. I argue that even if I concur with Frankfurt on his attack of the PAP, the ability to do otherwise is necessary for free will regardless.

Reasons-Responsiveness Condition

Given that the PAP cannot support Jones having free will, then there must be another account for why Jones was still morally responsible. Fischer and Ravizza (2008) argue for reasons-responsiveness, claiming that Jones' freedom depends on whether he is responsive to rational reasons, that is, whether he possesses a mechanism that can modify behaviour based on different rational reasons recognised. Jones' volition to act the way he did, say to shoot Smith, is free if he would have decided not to shoot Smith if he had pertinent reasons not to (McKenna, 2013). Suppose Smith was actually Jones' father, and this knowledge is a rational reason to which Jones' mechanism will react (by deciding not to shoot Smith). Then, Jones is free-willed, and thereby morally responsible for his actions, even if this knowledge never occurs to him, and he ends up shooting Smith under Black's scrutiny but without Black's interference. Had Black actually interfered when Jones decided not to shoot Smith, Jones' mechanism would then have lost his reasons-responsiveness due to coercion, as regardless of any reason presented to him (McKenna, 2000), he would still have shot Smith.

Ability to Do Otherwise

I contend that Fischer's proposal of reasons-responsiveness is ultimately still built upon the ability to do otherwise, insofar as there can be multiple notions of such ability, and that the notion of ability to do otherwise relevant to compatibilism does not entail "the possession of an opportunity" (Kittle, 2015, p. 87) that is executable. It is often argued that this notion should be an externalist one, such that the ability to do otherwise is exercisable in an accessible world (Robb, 2020). I contend, however, that with such an interpretation that is by definition already incompatible with determinism, the compatibilists end up defending a rather strawman argument against the necessity of alternate possibilities.

Given this distinction, I argue that the reactivity of Jones' mechanism can be interpreted as a sort of hypothetical ability of Jones' mechanism to do otherwise in "worlds in which [his] deliberative mechanism remains operative" (McKenna, 2000, p. 95). Black not intervening in the real world allows the existence of hypothetical worlds that "filter out any counterfactual interveners" (McKenna, 2000, p. 95), in which his mechanism does not lose its reason-responsiveness, and would still have the ability to react according to different reasons, that would thus allow Jones to do otherwise based on his reason in these hypothetical worlds. Therefore, though Jones, strictly speaking, does not have the ability to act otherwise with executable opportunities, it is this sort of hypothetical, psychological and internalist ability to do otherwise, that Jones' mechanism must possess when it is operative, that allows Jones to act freely.

Conclusion

I have thus argued that even if compatibilist free will necessitates not the externalist kind of alternate possibilities assumed by the PAP, it nevertheless necessitates the internalist kind. Given that compatibilist and libertarian free will are both necessarily contingent on a certain species of ability to do otherwise — hypothetical or exercisable — and that both constitute kinds of free will sufficient for moral responsibility, I conclude that the ability to do otherwise is necessary for the kind of free will that is sufficient for moral responsibility.
