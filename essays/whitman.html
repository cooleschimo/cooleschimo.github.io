Singing the Oppressor's Song

Comparing the Détournement of Body Politics of the Slave and the Woman in "I Sing the Body Electric"

"Gentlemen, look on this curious creature." (85) With this unsettling invitation, Walt Whitman thrusts us into the dehumanizing world of a slave auction and steps into the voice of oppression — but only for the sake of dismantling it from within. In Section 7 of "I Sing the Body Electric," Whitman's speaker hijacks the dehumanizing language of the slave auction, transforming it into a fierce declaration of human dignity. Similarly in Section 5, the speaker jumps into the titillated male gaze, describing his strong desire for "the divine nimbus" as "it attracts with a fierce undeniable attraction" (47-48, italics added). As Whitman himself had claimed, "Leaves of Grass is essentially a woman's book" (Mullins, 1993), suggesting that his appropriation of the male perspective was a deliberate attempt to empower women. "I Sing the Body Electric" thus can be seen as Whitman's poetic manifesto for emancipating the marginalized communities of his time. Yet Whitman treads a fine line between reclaiming their inherent worth, and reinscribing their position in entrenched hierarchies. When is it détournement…and when is it simply endorsement? In this essay, I explore the contrast between Whitman's treatment of the slave—particularly the male slave—and the woman, as well as the effectiveness of his poetics in truly challenging the ingrained politics of antebellum America and defending the innate worth of humanity.

To begin, Whitman positions his speaker alongside oppression—the auctioneer and the male gaze—when writing about both the slave and the woman. However, while he subverts the auctioneer's voice through satire, he seems to reinforce the male gaze without similar mockery. The first stanza of Section 7, which describes a slave auction, opens with the speaker actively positioning himself alongside the auctioneer: "I help the auctioneer" (84). The present active voice ostensibly portrays the speaker as an eager participant in the slavery practice, especially since "the sloven does not half know his business" (84). By calling the auctioneer a "sloven," a term that connotes carelessness and crudity—far from our typical impressions of a polished businessman—the speaker criticizes his professional inadequacy. However, the speaker's seeming act of assistance to the auctioneer soon reveals itself as caustic satire in the next stanza: "Whatever the bids of the bidders, they cannot be high enough for him." (86) Though the speaker simultaneously adopts a typical auction house hyperbole, he is rather asserting the impossibility of putting a price on human worth. We thus realize that the earlier claim against the auctioneer "not half know[ing] his business" carries a double meaning: while appearing to merely criticize the auctioneer's salesmanship, the speaker actually condemns his ignorance of a human's immense and immeasurable value. The speaker satirizes how the auctioneer has in fact catastrophically undervalued his 'merchandise' by seeing only its market worth. The speaker's "help" thus emerges as derisive mockery — the auctioneer fails both as a salesman and as a moral being because he cannot recognize that the real value of a human cannot be priced, infinitely exceeding that which he is selling the slave at.

In fact, we soon see the speaker fully inhabiting the auctioneer's voice in "Gentlemen, look on this curious creature," and later even commanding the audience to "Examine these limbs" (85), as if presenting a catalog of features for inspection and quality assurance. In this commercial display, he echoes the cold objectification of slave auctions that debases the enslaved person into nothing more than colored body parts: "limbs, red, black, or white" (91) — an inventory that erases individuality and personhood. However, the speaker then immediately fractures this line with ellipses, visually and rhythmically breaking away from that degrading categorization to assert that these very limbs are in fact ". . . . very cunning in tendon and nerve." (91) Though he maintains the auctioneer's anatomical language—one could even say he further dismembers the slave into "tendon" and "nerve"—he reroutes these objective terms by bestowing on them agency and intelligence through the adjective "cunning."

Yet when the speaker turns his gaze toward women's bodies in section 5 of "I Sing the Body Electric," he does not push back against the male perspective as he did against the auctioneer:

This is the female form, A divine nimbus exhales from it from head to foot, It attracts with fierce undeniable attraction. (46-48)

Reminiscent of the dehumanizing tone in "Gentlemen, look on this curious creature," the first line of this section "This is the female form" presents the female body as a specimen to be viewed, rather than the woman as a human. There is an immediate objectification of the woman as an "it" instead of a "she" — the woman is not a person but an "it" that "exhales" and "attracts." The active verbs "exhales" and "attracts" endow women with a false sense of agency, suggesting that a woman cannot participate actively in the world beyond breathing or seducing. The speaker thus conceives of the woman, and in particular her body, as an object that exists solely in the sphere of the man's perception. However, where his "help" to the auctioneer reveals itself as satire, his adoption of the male perspective involves no similar critique. In fact, the speaker even appears to endorse it, describing himself as "no more than a helpless vapor" before the woman. This proclaimed helplessness merely serves to emphasize male desire, and reaction, as the central experience of the section, where "all falls aside but myself and it". The existence of "it" is tied to that of the male observer; in fact, "it" also comes secondary to the male ("myself"), implicitly suggested by the ordering of pronouns. Thus even in claimed submission, the male gaze remains dominant. The poem focuses on the male observer's experience, rather than the woman's existence independent of being observed.

The voyeuristic scope of the speaker's gaze—spanning "from head to foot"—also catalogs the entire female body as a visual object for consumption, just as the auctioneer did. Yet while he employs the adjective "cunning" to affirm the slave's inner capabilities, his descriptors for the woman's body fail to empower women to the same effect. The speaker characterizes the female body with a "divine nimbus": though this elevates the female form to something sacred, the diaphanous imagery of clouds implied by "nimbus" also simultaneously suggests fragility. To label feminine delicacy as something "divine" thus aestheticizes and mystifies it as something heavenly — far from challenging patriarchal views of women's weakness. Even the apparent strength the speaker attributes to the female form—"fierce undeniable attraction"—is not her own power but rather the effect she produces on the male observer. The fierceness belongs not to the woman herself but to the attraction she generates. She does not act but is acted upon; her body does not desire but is desired. Thus, we see that while Whitman's speaker actively undermines the auctioneer, his portrayal of the woman on the other hand seems to feed into androcentrism.

At this juncture, I must note that though I will later contend that Whitman achieves the ideological emancipation of the slave and the woman to different degrees, it is not the ostensible distinction between his treatment of the auctioneer's and the male perspectives that begets such a conclusion. This divergence implies only that Whitman adopts differing approaches to détournement: the technique of rerouting and subverting the oppressor's language. For both the slave and the woman, Whitman still endeavors to transform their oppressor's voice into a source of empowerment. For the slave in section 7, such enfranchisement emerges more evidently from the central tension between two similar but ultimately different catalogs of the body — one that dehumanizes ("limbs, red, black, or white") and another that empowers:

Exquisite senses, lifelit eyes, pluck, volition, Flakes of breastmuscle, pliant backbone and neck, flesh not flabby, goodsized arms and legs, (93-94)

Through détournement, the speaker transforms typical auction house rhetoric into a powerful reclamation of human dignity. This is seen in how the speaker uses modifiers to elevate mere bodily lexicon into markers of humanity in stanza four. "Life-lit eyes" turn mere facial organs into the windows of an illuminated and vivacious soul. The "backbone", which should be firm and strong, is forcibly made "pliant", capturing a heartbreaking human reality of having to yield one's natural dignity in slavery. Similarly, in "flakes of breast-muscle", the word "flakes" suggests delicateness and fragility, undercutting the typical association of "muscle" with power to reveal the tender humanity beneath a corporeal exterior. Within this list he also mixes in qualities of inner character and strength such as "pluck" and "volition", as well as purely physical traits such as "flesh not flabby, goodsized arms and legs." In doing so he transcends but also acknowledges the physical body, refusing to let either dimension—physical or spiritual—be divorced from the other in service of the slave trade's dehumanizing agenda. By weaponizing the very language of objectification—detailed somatic inventories—to assert the fundamental impossibility of reducing a human being to a collection of parts, he transforms the dehumanizing advertisement of a slave into an adamant refusal to commodify the priceless human.

In section 5, contrastingly, while Whitman's speaker similarly cataloged the female body, it is clear that it is not his description of their body that empowers them. "Hair, bosom, hips, bend of legs, negligent falling hands—all diffused... mine too diffused" (52) — where the speaker endows the slave's body with vitality and humanity, the woman's body parts are mostly objects of desire with no adjective ("bosom, hips, bend of legs"). Otherwise, they are simply languid: her "hands" are "negligent" and "falling," the double layering of adjectives conveying a heavy sense of passivity, reinforcing traditional gender norms of female subservience. Additionally, unlike the slave's modifiers that grant him specific qualities and distinct personhood, the key modifier on the woman's body is just "diffused," alluding to the dispersion of the woman's identity. The "diffused" woman lacks a concrete self-definition: her identity blurs not just within her own body, but also into the male observer. As both parties intermingle during intimacy, the sensual imagery of intermingled body parts evoked by "all diffused... mine too diffused" once again establishes the woman's identity as intertwined with male desire instead of individual and autonomous. This diffusion thus diminishes the woman's ability to claim the kind of specific, innate human qualities that Whitman so powerfully attributes to the slave.

However, while Whitman's speaker does not 'rectify' his adopted male gaze the same way he 'rectifies' the auctioneer's voice, by characterizing the woman through such explicitly lustful male desire, he challenges nineteenth-century norms that demanded female sexual modesty and restraint. In fact, we can read the section through the lens of a literary theory that French feminist theorist Hélène Cixous' coined as l'écriture feminine — a kind of "writing within the 'phallocentric' tradition," but which subverts its logic "by its focus on the female body, glorying in a femininity too long repressed." (Olsen, 2004) Whitman's writing strikingly possesses the essential characteristics of l'écriture feminine: "multiplicity, diffuseness, and openness—experienced through the body and in particular through orgasmic utterance" (Mullins, 1993), and the focus on the female body and its experiences. Indeed Section 5 of the poem conveys all of the above of the woman, not only directly through the word "diffused" but also through the imagery of the "nimbus," "vapor," "atmosphere" and "fringed clouds," as well as the multiple lists of abstract, open-ended ideas: "Books, art, religion, time", "Mad filaments, ungovernable shoots," and "heaven" and "hell." (50-51) Whitman's explicit depiction of sexual intimacy in "loveflesh swelling and deliciously aching, / Limitless limpid jets of love hot and enormous... quivering jelly of love... white-blow and delirious juice" (53-54) is the pinnacle of the "orgasmic" quality that Cixous celebrates in feminine writing, and especially paints an epicurean picture of indulgence and pleasure with gastronomic diction of "deliciously", "jelly" and "juice."

Returning to the "diffused" woman, we see that she is more liberated than subjugated. As Cixous articulates, her diffused identity allows her to embrace all parts of her body, including its sexuality and carnal qualities, as a "moving, limitlessly changing ensemble." (Cixous et al., 1976) Though Whitman predates Cixous by over a century, his elicitation of women's sexual liberation through appropriating the male desire is what Cixous would celebrate as freeing the woman's body from "that stupid sexual modesty" (Cixous et al., 1976) through writing. Therefore, though Whitman empowers the slave through direct rerouting of the auctioneer's rhetoric, he liberates the woman by feeding her the male gaze as fodder for realizing her sexuality, transforming it into her exact source of power.

However, while I believe that Whitman emancipates both the slave and the woman—each to a certain extent—through hijacking their oppressor's language, he is ultimately more effective in affirming the slave's innate worth more than the woman's. In Section 7 of the poem, the mounting intensity of the speaker's list of modified body parts eventually culminates in "wonders within there yet" (95), after spanning the entire stanza in a single sentence. An emphasis is made strong through its repeated stressed-unstressed rhythm and alliterative 'w' — the speaker thus stresses that no amount of external physical examination can capture the full intrinsic humanity of the person. The word "yet" is especially crucial: though these "wonders" remain unseen by the bidders who only scrutinize his body, they exist in his soul, waiting to be recognized. Indeed, the speaker not only highlights the existence of the slave's innate but unappreciated worth, but also adjures the audience to peer past surface observations: "They shall be stript that you may see them." (92) While the word "stript" suggests a violent subjection of the slave to an invasive glare on the auction block, the line is also simultaneously a request—a plea even—for the audience to "see" the human within. We can even return to the speaker's previous usage of imperatives in the poem, "look on" and "examine." They are more than just the auctioneer's instructions, or the speaker's sardonic imitation of them. They are the speaker's call to action for the audience to truly see what superficial glances have missed — and also Whitman's call to action for the reader as he wrote in an age of rampant slavery.

Yet when we compare these "wonders within" to the woman's "bath of birth" (59), we discover a significantly limited view of female worth. While the enslaved man possesses multiple dimensions—intellectual capacity ("allbaffling brain" (89)), ambitions ("passions and desires...all reachings and aspirations" (97)) and spiritual strength ("pluck, volition" (93))—the woman in Section 5 is valued only as "the bearer of the great fruit which is immortality" (65), a glorified allusion to her child-bearing abilities. The speaker reminds women that their ability "to conceive daughters as well as sons" (63) is their "privilege." (60) Invoking the idea of "privilege" in stanzas overwhelmingly focused on fertility frames childbearing as a special honor that women should naturally celebrate when it is as much a limitation as a blessing. The inequality between men and women becomes even clearer when comparing parallel passages: Whitman's speaker emphatically declares that the enslaved man is "the father of those who shall be fathers in their turns, / In him the start of populous states and rich republics." (99-100) However, the enslaved woman in Section 9 is merely "the teeming mother of mothers, / She is the bearer of them that shall grow and be mates to the mothers." (105-106) The enslaved man's significance extends to political and historical impact ("populous states and rich republics"), while the woman's significance still remains confined to reproduction and the domestic sphere ("the bearer of them…mates to the mothers"). Whitman's limited view of female worth ultimately endorses, and even reinforces, conventional gender hierarchies — in stark contrast to the multitudes of potential he envisions for the enslaved man.

In "I Sing the Body Electric", Whitman's contrasting treatments of the slave and the woman reveal both the revolutionary potential and inherent limitations of his poetic vision of détournement. Similar to the sentimental literature that dominated nineteenth-century America, a genre in which authors appealed to the emotions of their target audience to spur social reform—for instance writing in a manner to "make readers 'feel right' and assume an antislavery position" (Weinstein, 2007)—Whitman worked within the dominant ideology of society and subverted it from within to inspire change. In fact, Section 7 achieves this exactly; it turns the dehumanizing gaze of the audience—and even of the poem's readers—back upon itself to reveal a fundamental moral bankruptcy. In the same vein, we consider Whitman's adoption of the androcentric view of women: had Whitman been writing for a male audience, his emphasis on women's reproductive capacity might be understood as challenging men to respect this power. However his direct address to women—"Be not ashamed women" (60)—suggests he intended female readers to find affirmation in his words. Such is the irony: his affirmation to women comes through celebrating the very biological function that nineteenth-century society was reducing women to — an endorsement of the very structures of inequality he sought to subvert through détournement. "I Sing the Body Electric" is most certainly not "the cry of the right and wrong of the woman sex" as Whitman had envisioned (Mullins, 1993), but it is nonetheless a sincere and powerful effort towards dismantling social hierarchies. The poem is thus testament to how even the most revolutionary poetic voices may be constrained by their historical and social position—a white male poet in a patriarchal society.
