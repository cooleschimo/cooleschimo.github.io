Singing the Auctioneer's Song

Whitman's Détournement of the Slave Auction in "I Sing the Body Electric"

"Gentlemen, look on this curious creature." (23) With this unsettling invitation, Walt Whitman thrusts us into the dehumanizing world of a slave auction and steps into the voice of oppression — but only for the sake of dismantling it from within. In "A slave at auction" in "I Sing the Body Electric," Whitman's speaker hijacks the dehumanizing language of the slave auction, transforming it into a fierce declaration of human dignity and worth.

In the first stanza, the poem opens with the speaker actively positioning himself alongside the auctioneer: "I help the auctioneer" (2). The present active voice ostensibly portrays the speaker as an eager participant in the slavery practice, especially since "the sloven does not half know his business" (2). By calling the auctioneer a "sloven," a term that connotes carelessness and crudity — far from our typical impressions of a polished businessman — the speaker criticizes his professional inadequacy. However, the speaker's seeming act of assistance to the auctioneer soon reveals itself as caustic satire in the next stanza: "Whatever the bids of the bidders, they cannot be high enough for him." (4) Though the speaker simultaneously adopts a typical auction house hyperbole, he is rather asserting the impossibility of putting a price on human worth. We thus realize that the earlier claim against the auctioneer "not half know[ing] his business" carries a double meaning: while appearing to merely criticize the auctioneer's salesmanship, the speaker actually condemns his ignorance of a human's immense and immeasurable value. The speaker satirizes how the auctioneer has in fact catastrophically undervalued his 'merchandise' by seeing only its market worth. The speaker's "help" thus emerges as derisive mockery — the auctioneer fails both as a salesman and as a moral being because he cannot recognize that the real value of a human is unpriced.

In fact, we see the speaker fully inhabiting the auctioneer's voice in "Gentlemen, look on this curious creature," and later even commanding the audience to "Examine these limbs" (9), as if presenting a catalog of features for inspection and quality assurance. In this commercial display, he echoes the cold objectification of slave auctions that debases the enslaved person into nothing more than colored body parts: "limbs, red, black, or white" (9) — an inventory that erases individuality and personhood. However, the speaker then immediately fractures this line with ellipses, visually and rhythmically breaking away from that degrading categorization to assert that these very limbs are in fact ". . . . very cunning in tendon and nerve." (9) Though he maintains the auctioneer's anatomical language — one could even say he further dismembers the slave into "tendon" and "nerve" — he reroutes these objective terms by bestowing on them agency and intelligence through the adjective "cunning." The poem's central tension thus emerges precisely in these similar but ultimately different catalogs of the body: one that dehumanizes and another that empowers. Through this détournement — the technique of rerouting and subverting an oppressor's language — the speaker transforms typical auction house rhetoric into a powerful reclamation of human dignity.

This is seen in how the speaker uses modifiers to elevate mere bodily lexicon into markers of humanity in stanza four. "Life-lit eyes" (11) turn mere facial organs into the windows of an illuminated and vivacious soul. The "backbone", which should be firm and strong, is forcibly made "pliant" (12), capturing a heartbreaking human reality of having to yield one's natural dignity in slavery. Similarly, in "flakes of breast-muscle" (12), the word "flakes" suggests delicateness and fragility, undercutting the typical association of "muscle" with power to reveal the tender humanity beneath a corporeal exterior. Within this list he also mixes in qualities of inner character and strength such as "pluck" and "volition" (11), as well as purely physical traits such as "flesh not flabby, goodsized arms and legs." (12) In doing so he transcends but also acknowledges the physical body, refusing to let either dimension — physical or spiritual — be divorced from the other in service of the slave trade's dehumanizing agenda.

The mounting intensity of this list, contained in a single, sustained sentence spanning the entire stanza, eventually culminates in "wonders within there yet" (13), an emphasis made strong through its repeated stressed-unstressed rhythm and alliterative 'w' — the speaker stresses that no amount of external physical examination can capture the full intrinsic humanity of the person. The word "yet" is especially crucial: though these "wonders" remain unseen by the bidders who only scrutinize his body, they exist in his soul, waiting to be recognized. Indeed, the speaker not only highlights the existence of the slave's innate but unappreciated worth, but also adjures the audience to peer past surface observations: "They shall be stript that you may see them." (10) While the word "stript" suggests a violent subjection of the slave to an invasive glare on the auction block, the line is also simultaneously a request — a plea even — for the audience to "see" the human within. We can even return to the speaker's previous usage of imperatives in the poem, "look on" and "examine." They are more than just the auctioneer's instructions, or the speaker's sardonic imitation of them. They are the speaker's call to action for the audience to truly see what superficial glances have missed — and also Whitman's call to action for the reader as he wrote in an age of rampant slavery.

The speaker thus weaponizes the very language of objectification — detailed somatic inventories — to assert the fundamental impossibility of reducing a human being to a collection of parts, transforming the dehumanizing advertisement into an adamant refusal to commodify the priceless human. Whitman's poem does more than simply condemn slavery; it turns the dehumanizing gaze of the audience — and even of the poem's readers — back upon itself to reveal a fundamental moral bankruptcy.
