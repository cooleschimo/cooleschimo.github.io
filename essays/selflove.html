Self-love Shouldn't Be Killing You

Back in the 2000s, we've got Barbie-bodies and size zero glamour girls plastered on billboards and cover pages of magazines. Angry mobs decried these unrealistic beauty standards, putting up a battle against weight-bias, chanting their go-to mantra "Love yourself!" and lambasting diet culture in the form of fat-acceptance and body-positivity movements. Today, it seems that the pendulum has swung from one extreme to the other. We now see obese influencers speckled everywhere on social media, advocating the new normal of being Plus-Sized. But why do we idolise them as vanguards while we condemn skinny models for causing eating disorders? Self-love doesn't call for normalising obesity, which is as unhealthy as perpetuating unrealistic beauty standards.

To begin, I agree that it is of utmost importance to champion self-love and confidence when it comes to our bodies. In this world, we live and die by societal divides of race, gender, class, and now, even body size. It is certainly a priority to advocate the equal treatment of plus-sized people and to embrace bodies of various shapes and sizes. It's admirable that people are crushing the beauty norms of society instead of getting crushed by them. It's brilliant to see people confident in their own body, head up and heart strong. And it would be wonderful to have a more welcoming society of diverse body types, the raison d'être of the fat-acceptance movement.

However, while we applaud the promotion of self-love and the empowerment of obese individuals, we must face the problem of obesity squarely. We are in the midst of an escalating epidemic of "globesity" that kills 2.8 million every year due to complications like heart disease, diabetes and cancer. It is imperative that we terminate this worrying trend of obesity before the lives of the next generation are terminated by it. But the fat-acceptance movement is straying away from its original intentions of empowerment. Instead, they are normalising, even celebrating, obesity, and this is when the movement becomes problematic.

Prominent figures in the fat-acceptance movement like supermodel Tess Holliday and pop-singer Lizzo, both of whom are obese, emphasise how it's "okay" to be fat. Sure, a few fat rolls here and there won't hurt, but these plus-sized influencers are placed on a pedestal. Their appearance in the media isn't as simple as representing a common demographic in society — it is also endorsing the notion that obesity is normal, and one should even be proud of it (as suggested by the term "fat pride"). There is a fine line between accepting an individual for who he/she is, which we should always do, and accepting obesity — obesity isn't an identity; it's a condition with harmful consequences. The "empowerment" and "self-love" that the movement is supposed to encourage has morphed into a travesty that assures obese individuals that their status quo is just right, indirectly persuading them to continue with their unhealthy lifestyle. This dangerously decreases the health awareness of obesity-related consequences.

Many body-positivists would also view health-related advice as hateful rhetoric. Admittedly, there are many pejoratives surrounding obesity. Fatphobia is detrimental to one's well-being especially when they become an object for public comment or derision. However, raising health awareness is not to be confused with fat-shaming. This year, celebrity Jillian Michaels was slammed for saying that we should celebrate Lizzo's music, not her body — there's nothing amazing about diabetes. To critics, Michaels shouldn't have assumed that an overweight person would suffer complications as that perpetuated a stigma against obesity. Their flimsy defence was: It's possible to be "fat but fit". While it's possible for someone obese to have a clean bill of health, it's undeniable that obesity increases the risk of certain illnesses. And it's not patronising to point that out. Lack of health awareness and self-denial is as harmful as any litany of body-shaming insults.

At the same time, the body-positivity community witnesses a lot of hypocrisy — when activist leaders lose weight. When influencer JennyLee Molina decided to drop 80 pounds due to potential diabetes, body-positivist Tess Holliday unfollowed her. Similarly, pop-singer Adele, who previously embraced her plus-sized body, was labelled "hypocritical" after losing 100 pounds. Yet the real hypocrisy lies in how the movement is no longer about self-love and being comfortable in the body you choose to be in — weight loss is now a losing battle and "fat pride" is the holy grail.

Obesity is a sensitive topic. Teetering on the fine line between fatphobia and health awareness, the globesity epidemic is indubitably hard to tackle. It is pivotal that obese individuals find their confidence and call out discrimination, but while we absolutely condemn fat-shaming and champion self-love, there should be no place for misleading movements that normalise obesity. True self-love should never have to come at the expense of our health.
