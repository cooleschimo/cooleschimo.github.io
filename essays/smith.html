Mental and Physical Subsistence for All

A Smithian Critique of the Underprovision of Art in the Free Market

1   Labour and the Free Market

"The annual labour of every nation is the fund which originally supplies it with all the necessaries and conveniences of life" (xxiii) — the foundation of all economic value, according to Smith, lies in labour. Yet while labour is a common endowment among nations, Smith notes that even nations similar in their levels of skill and dexterity in labour diverge significantly in their "general conduct or direction of it" (xxv), each prioritising certain industries over others. These differences lead to vastly different amounts of "necessaries and conveniences" the economy can supply its people — not all "equally favourable." (xxv) As such, Smith is particularly critical of such purposeful, artificial allocation of labour, especially condemning the policy of Europe on the inequalities they caused upon contravening the "perfect liberty" of the market (136). However, while Smith may not agree with European policy on how labour is deployed, they concur on one thing: art. The policy of Europe had favoured the arts (xxv), and Smith, rather interestingly, had similarly advocated for subsidising art.

In this essay, I explore why Smith believes art deserves an exception from the free market, defining subsidy as a cash transfer given to either the producer or the consumer. I will first establish—with Smith's conception of labour—what art is and its value. Next, I argue that Smith's economic framework suggests that art is a merit good that justifies public support, but I will also acknowledge the drawbacks of subsidising art. However, I contend that insofar as government intervention is carefully designed to complement market mechanisms while ensuring that art's social and economic benefits reach all ranks of society, art subsidies ultimately pave the way to greater equality and thereby prosperity in society.

2   Productive and Unproductive Labors of Art

At the cornerstone of all economic activity is Smith's distinction between productive and unproductive labour, the former which "realises itself in some particular subject or vendible commodity" (360) and the latter which does not. Productive labor thus "produces a value" through its production of a commodity, whereas unproductive labor "adds to the value of nothing." (360)

In this essay, I define art to comprise both performing arts, such as theatre and music, and visual arts, such as painting and sculpting. Performing art is thus what Smith labels as unproductive labour — after all, "the declamation of the actor, the harangue of the orator, or the tune of the musician…perishes in the very instant of its production" (361), leaving nothing that can be exchanged for other commodities or services. On the other hand, visual artists create works that "[last] for some time at least after that labour is past" (360), thus "adding to the value of the materials which he works upon" (360) to produce the artwork when it is sold and earns a profit. Therefore, in Smith's economy, art involves both unproductive and productive labour, which I term "unproductive art" and "productive art" respectively in this essay.

3   Art as a Merit Good

With this definition of artistic labour in mind, I argue that art can be seen as a merit good with positive externalities that benefit the greater society beyond the immediate consumer, according to Smith's assessment of the value of unproductive and productive labour. However, unproductive and productive art differ in the kinds of value they bring to society.

3.1   Unproductive Art

Prima facie, it is easy to dismiss the "players, buffoons, musicians, opera-singers [and] opera-dancers," (361) whose work seldom leaves "any trace of value" (361), much "like that of menial servants" who drain their master's wealth (360). However, Smith also deliberately elevates these labourers as some of the "most respectable orders in the society." (361) While his definition of unproductive labour makes clear that performing arts do not directly contribute to the economy, it also stresses that nonetheless, they have "a certain value, regulated by the very same principles which regulate that of every other sort of labour." This value is non-material; existing not in a commodity, but in the wellbeing of society. He observes that "great labour" naturally creates "a great desire of relaxation"—irresistible even—which requires relief through "indulgence", "dissipation and diversion." (94) In an economy characterised by tedious repetitive work stemming from the nature of the division of labour (9), art is an outlet for workers to "indulge" in some sort of respite — only when each individual worker is mentally and emotionally healthy can the wellbeing and productivity of society improve as a whole.

3.2   Productive Art

On the other hand, the value of productive art is encapsulated by the value of its commodity. Smith separates a commodity's value into "value in use" and "value in exchange", the former being the object's utility, and the latter its purchasing power (31). If unproductive art could be treated as a commodity, it would have immense "value-in-use" due to its welfare benefits and little "value-in-exchange" due to its ephemeral nature. Productive art, then, is the opposite: it has high exchange value and little use value. Just like a diamond, it is not very useful in and of itself, but commands "a very great quantity of other goods" in exchange (32). Artworks are seen as durable, valuable goods: a man who spends his wealth "collecting books, statues, pictures" (377) would enjoy increasing magnificence and wealth (378). However, the value of productive art also extends beyond the individual — Smith recognises art as a positional good, the accumulation of which is "favourable..to the opulence of an individual…likewise to that of a nation." (378) Citing the examples of Versailles to France, and Stowe and Wilton to England, he remarks that "great collections of books, statues, pictures...are frequently both an ornament and an honour, not only to the neighbourhood, but to the whole country to which they belong."(379) The benefits of productive art scale from private to public advantage, generating positive externalities that enhance national prestige and cultural capital.

Therefore, Smith portrays art in both its unproductive and productive forms as a merit good that substantially betters society.

4   Correcting Market Failure with Subsidies

Despite art's vital contributions to society's mental welfare and glory, Smith's framework reveals why the free market alone struggles to attain the optimal provision of both unproductive and productive art: art is underproduced due to its unprofitability as a business, and underconsumed due to its high prices. These market failures not only fail to clear the socially optimal quantity of art in the market, but also per se lead to exacerbated inequality across society — subsidies are thus all the more pivotal to correct these failures.

4.1   Underproduction of Art: An Unprofitable Trade

The first market failure in art lies in its systematic underproduction due to its inherent unprofitability. This unprofitability manifests differently for unproductive and productive art. For unproductive artists, the primary problem lies in their inability to receive a natural wage based on Smith's fundamental principle that "the produce of labour constitutes the natural recompence or wages of labour." (73) Unlike manufacturers who create vendible goods, we cannot directly evaluate the monetary value of a, say, theatre actor's labour. Hence, unproductive artists are instead sustained by the surplus of the annual produce that is "over and above their necessary subsistence" (363) — which, by its nature as surplus, is never certain and always limited. After all, "no part of the annual produce…is ever directed towards maintaining unproductive hands, till after it has put into motion its full complement of productive labour" (363), placing unproductive artists at the end of the economic queue. At the same time, this income is also highly vulnerable to economic downturns, with Smith noting that "the rise and fall in the wages of labour" is aligned with "the increasing or declining state of the wealth of the society." (100) The triple constraints of limited expenditure on unproductive services, their subordinate position to productive capital, and their strong dependence on economic fluctuations thus leave unproductive artists without a secure means of livelihood, causing many artistic enterprises to fail.

The worry of unprofitability extends to productive art as well, but in a different manner. While productive artists can better determine their wages through tangible commodities, they can still face constraints on their market. Smith explains that in "lone houses and very small villages," rural areas which comprise the majority of a country, "every farmer must be butcher, baker, and brewer, for his own family…we can scarce expect to find even a smith, a carpenter, or a mason, within less than twenty miles of another of the same trade" (19). This limited demand is problematic because the extent of the division of labour "must always be limited by...the extent of the market" (19) — as a highly specialised profession, when the market is small, artists cannot justify specialising in their craft when they simply are unable to trade "all that surplus part of the produce" beyond their own consumption; they must instead juggle art and other necessary jobs — or even give up art altogether. As Smith mentions, "it is better...to play for nothing, than to work for nothing." (365) Without market incentives, artists end up abandoning their craft, leading to the fall in production. This decline is not merely theoretical; Smith references Italy as a cautionary tale. While Italy commands "some sort of veneration" by its number of monuments, he observes that "the genius which planned them seems to be extinguished, perhaps from not having the same employment." (379) This suggests that even regions with rich artistic traditions can lose their creative capacity when market conditions fail to support artistic specialization and development.

4.1.1   Safeguarding the Fair Value of Artistic Labour

With artists struggling to stay afloat in a free market, safeguarding the fair value of artistic labour has only one solution: subsidy. First and foremost, Smith emphasises a fundamental necessity: "a man must always live by his work, and his wages must at least be sufficient to maintain him." (77) As Smith argues, it is "equity" that workers should receive enough from their labor "to be themselves tolerably well fed, clothed, and lodged" (90) — artists deserve to sustain themselves through their work as much as any other profession.

Smith even clearly justifies why artists deserve wages higher than mere subsistence. He argues that labour's real price should reflect "the toil and trouble of acquiring it" (33) — which for art would be rather high given the "uncommon degree of dexterity and ingenuity" it needs (53). A premium is justified because "such talents can seldom be acquired but in consequence of long application," making their higher value "no more than a reasonable compensation for the time and labour which must be spent in acquiring them." (53) Artistic excellence requires a large investment in cultivation — an investment that necessitates secure and adequate compensation to make it worth their time and effort. Thus, subsidies to producers of art ensure fair compensation for artists, both protecting their livelihoods and facilitating their business, which eventually addresses the broader problem of art's underproduction.

4.2   Underconsumption of Art: An Exclusive Luxury

The second market failure in art is its underconsumption as art is largely inaccessible to the broader population. For productive art in urban areas, this inequitable access stems primarily from artificially inflated prices caused by monopolies. Smith explains that "the exclusive privileges of corporations, statutes of apprenticeship…restrain…the competition to a smaller number than…otherwise", which "keep up the market price of particular commodities above the natural price." (70) This is explained by Smith's price adjustment mechanism in a free market: as there are few producers and even fewer goods, those "willing to pay…cannot be supplied with the quantity which they want." (64) The scarcity triggers a competition where "some of them will be willing to give more," (64) driving market prices up. Even worse, the extent of this price inflation depends on "the greatness of the deficiency" and "the wealth and wanton luxury of the competitors" (64) — both of which are exceptionally pronounced for art, where wealthy collectors compete for limited, rare pieces. The result is that productive art becomes a luxury confined to "a man of fortune" who can "spend his revenue...in adorning his house or his country villa." (377)

For unproductive art, the access barrier stems from what was established earlier: it can only be sustained by surplus profit from productive labor. With the middle and lower classes having limited surplus after meeting basic needs, they can hardly afford any unproductive services. Therefore, regardless of productivity, art remains a privilege of the wealthy, culminating in a grave underconsumption that prevents it from fulfilling its broader social functions.

4.2.1   Democratising Access to Luxury

Subsidies, therefore, come into play again to combat market failure — this time to democratise access to art. By lowering the market price of art, without eating into the wages of the artists, subsidies can make art accessible to lower ranks of society not at the expense of the labourers' welfare. Instead of purely being a luxury available to the wealthy, middle and lower classes can also enjoy the decoration of their homes in intricate art as well as attend performances that provide essential relaxation — all the more beneficial to these lower classes who are typically involved in gruelling industrial work.

Increasing access to art is also not merely about individual benefits, but about fostering a more equitable society. When "luxury extends itself even to the lowest ranks of the people," (90) Smith takes this not to signal a spreading plague of indulgence, but rather as a strong evidence of progress — it proves that "real recompence...has augmented." (90) Wages at the lower echelons of society have risen enough to afford more than just necessities. When considering whether "this improvement in the circumstances of the lower ranks of the people" benefits society, he declares the answer "abundantly plain" — "what improves the circumstances of the greater part, can never be regarded as any inconvenience to the whole." (90) Smith believes that reducing inequality benefits everyone, positing that "no society can surely be flourishing and happy, of which the far greater part of the members are poor and miserable." (90) In the same vein, subsidies that make available art and its associated benefits to all ranks of society will allow for a truly "flourishing and happy" society as inequalities that hold back social progress diminish.

5   The Danger of Subsidies

However, as compelling as the reasons to subsidise art are, I recognise that Smith's economic framework also simultaneously highlights the risks of art subsidies: they could encourage wasteful resource allocation away from productive capital, or they could be captured by existing monopolies, failing to achieve their intended effects.

5.1   Proliferation of the Prodigal's Perversion

Art subsidies run the risk of inducing wasteful consumption of unproductive goods. Smith warns against the rise of the "prodigal", who "pays the wages of idleness with those funds which...had...been consecrated to the maintenance of industry" — he "perverts" the proper use of capital by spending excessively on unproductive performing arts (369). Ultimately at its baseline, performing arts remain an unproductive economic activity that is "maintained by the annual produce of the land and labour of the country" (361), from which funds are diverted to such unproductive labour instead of invested in capital, will significantly deplete the nation's productive potential. Smith cites historical evidence of the failures of economies that prioritized unproductive over productive expenditure. He contrasts "mercantile and manufacturing towns" (365) where people are "industrious, sober, and thriving" with "Rome, Versailles, Compeigne, and Fontainbleau", towns characterised by revenue-spending, where people are "idle, dissolute, and poor." (366)

For productive art, Smith also draws an important distinction between durable art like statues and pictures that accumulate value for society, as compared to art forms like "baubles, ingenious trinkets." (377) He does not hesitate to censure it as "a trifling…base and selfish disposition" (380), for the consumption of frivolous objects of art has neither use-value nor exchange-value — such goods are short-lived and serve only individual opulence. Unconducive "to the increase of the public capital" (380), these types of art merely accumulate as transient private luxuries instead of circulating productively in the economy — and are thus no different from spending on "unproductive hands." (380)

The risk of prodigality is further amplified by what Smith identifies as a natural human predisposition toward unproductive expenditure: while surplus revenue could be spent on either productive or unproductive hands, people "seem, however, to have some predilection for the latter." (363) This tendency affects all social classes — from "the great lord" who "feeds generally more idle than industrious people," to even "the common workman": "if his wages are considerable…[he] may sometimes go to a play or a puppet-show, and so contribute his share towards maintaining one set of unproductive labourers." (363) Thus, making art too affordable through subsidies could exacerbate a tendency to overconsume unproductive services.

5.2   Elite Capture by Monopolies

If subsidising the consumer would risk overconsumption of unproductive services, subsidising the producer is also not without its drawbacks: existing monopolistic institutions may fully capture the effect of the subsidies instead of passing it on to the consumers. Smith has already identified that monopolies ensure that the market is "constantly under-stocked, by never fully supplying the effectual demand" (69) — if subsidies are implemented without addressing these underlying monopolistic structures, there is no guarantee that the monopolists do not pocket the sum, or simply supply even fewer commodities, so as to extract "the highest which can be squeezed out of the buyers." (69)

6   The Careful Employment of Subsidies

While I am cognisant of the risks of prodigal spending and elite capture, I argue that Smith's economic framework ultimately still supports art subsidies when properly implemented. The key lies in designing interventions that achieve the goals of ensuring fair compensation and democratising access, while minimising the associated dangers.

For unproductive art, I contend that subsidising the consumer directly and funding public art programs fit squarely into his economic ideology despite concerns over promoting indulgence. Funds will indeed inevitably be diverted from capital accumulation — but given Smith's own emphasis on the importance of society-wide happiness and a worker's wellbeing, it is evident that the social advantages far outweigh the economic costs. The risk of encouraging prodigal behavior is also reduced if subsidies are specifically targeted at raising the wages of the artist, or making public art more accessible by subsidising free performances and funding public operas and theatres, rather than supporting excessive luxury consumption of exclusive shows. For productive art, however, more careful implementation of subsidies is needed. Subsidies must be accompanied by antitrust laws to effectively lower art prices for consumers rather than being captured by monopolies. They should also target the promotion of durable artworks that contribute to lasting cultural and economic value, such as native and indigenous art or works dedicated to championing a social cause — instead of frivolous decorative trinkets of big name brands.

Through these carefully designed interventions, art subsidies can achieve Smith's vision of broader access while mitigating their pitfalls.

7   Equity and Prosperity

In this essay, I have shown that Smith's attitude toward the arts precludes hasty generalisations of the laissez-faire capitalist philosophy we often attribute to him. I have first demonstrated that both unproductive and productive art can be seen as merit goods with positive externalities, but critical market failures necessitate government intervention in allocating resources equitably in society. I then elucidated how subsidies, insofar as they are targeted and carefully designed, can still ensure fair compensation for artists and democratise access to art without leading to economic stagnation. Smith's advocacy for art subsidies is not mutually exclusive with a predominantly free market for other commodities of productive labour — after all, a nation's prosperity depends as much on the welfare of its labourers as on the freedom of its markets.
