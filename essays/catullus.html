If I could play with you — her sparrow

Distance, Desire and Dis-possession in Catullus II

The girl, her sparrow, her plaything — all of which Catullus loves from afar. In Peter Whigham's translation of "Catullus II," Catullus' tentative expression of desire for intimacy with Lesbia emerges from a carefully architectured structure of cautious diction, scattered spaces and shifting narrative perspectives. Through these elements, Catullus seeks to restrain his desire by creating distance: from Lesbia, from his longing, and even from his own identity. Yet despite — or perhaps because of — this carefully constructed separation, his desire does not diminish but paradoxically intensifies, becoming most perceptible in the very moments he attempts to evade it. How can we reconcile his attempts to temper desire with a yearning that only grows stronger with distance? Catullus struggles between control and confession — and this incongruity begins to appear in the early lines of the poem.

In the first few lines of "Catullus II," as the poem's speaker, Catullus explicitly names himself through the third-person perspective in "Catullus's desire" (3). He employs the possessive form "Catullus's," highlighting his possession of "desire." Yet, the choice of a proper noun in "Catullus" itself instead of a personal first-person pronoun "my" also suggests an intentional alienation of himself from his own identity and the "desire" that he harbors. The speaker tries to appear as a third-party, framing the desire as belonging to a distant "Catullus," rather than acknowledging it as his own in the first person. Ironically, by invoking his own name and using the possessive form, Catullus ultimately claims the desire as his own. Thus, in the exact same line there coexists two grammatical features that contradict each other's effects: the proper noun that suggests detachment, but also its possessiveness that suggests personal ownership. The syntax of this self-reflective phrase thus gives rise to a strong semantic tension between Catullus admitting that he yearns for something, and his active attempt to distance himself from such a yearning.

It is precisely this tension that is exhibited through Catullus' tentative articulation of his desire. "Catullus's desire / gleams," (3-4) the line break in this clause seemingly trying to cut off the manifestation of his desire. He tries to delay and impede its externalization on paper, pushing it as far as he can to the next line. This explains the long white space that follows the enjambed line, a literal and metaphorical hesitance — even reluctance — in his self-expression of desire. However, despite this double effort to engineer pauses in the poem, the "desire" emerges nonetheless — it presents itself after the white space in the singular verb "gleams." The placement and connotation of this verb even betrays the true intensity of his desire; the white space ironically highlights what is present in the vast stretch of nothingness: "gleams" — the first verb in the poem, and also the only word with an entire line to itself. The word "gleams" also denotes strength in intensity and longevity; Catullus characterizes his desire with the constant beaming of light. The present tense and active form of the verb then further emphasizes the current and actively-occurring nature of his longing. In fact, the connection of "gleams" to its temporal conjunction "when," instead of "if," concretizes the manifestation of his desire as an inevitable reality, not a possibility. His desire well exists and persists despite attempts to dampen it. We thus realize that what pushes the flighty lines around the page in cautious lexicon is merely a feigned sense of control. The strong vocabulary embedded in pensive, hesitant white spaces reveals a powerful desire that, despite Catullus' endeavors to remove himself from it, shines through self-restraint.

However, why does Catullus distance himself from the very desire he acknowledges as his own? Perhaps it is exactly because this abstract desire is the only thing he owns. The poem opens with a litany of possessive phrases — "Lesbia's sparrow! / Lesbia's plaything," "her lap…her breast" (1-3) — standing in stark contrast to Catullus' single possession: his "desire." The numerical juxtaposition of possessives underscores Catullus' non-possession: sure, he possesses a "desire" — but nothing of what is desired. A sense of impotence thus permeates Catullus' wishful entreaty: "if I could play with you / her sparrow" (12-13). Unlike the undeniable acknowledgement of his longing with "when," the choice of "if" confirms our suspicion that Catullus' desire remains unfulfilled: he is unable to play with "you / her sparrow." This state of non-possession is further emphasized through narrative perspective. Catullus refers to Lesbia only in third-person "her," the linguistic distance paralleling their corporeal distance. Yet we also see that his choice of pronoun could indicate a purposeful act of emotional distancing. Indeed, his relationship with "her" is as much the non-possessed as it is the dis-possessed. Faced with what he does not have, Catullus, albeit self-defeatingly, creates careful distance through language, perhaps an effort to alleviate the "sorrow" (14) of unsatiated "desire" — the only two nouns he possesses, rather dismally, in the poem.

Yet through the poem, we realize his attempts at removing himself from desire repeatedly falters. His language oscillates between vague abstraction and concrete specificity, suggesting a pattern of restraint and relapse. Early in the poem, while erogenous zones "lap" and "breast" (3) locate desire with anatomical precision, Catullus' own desire merely "gleams," an abstract intransitive verb with neither direction nor object — a cautious attempt to weaken the portrayal of desire. Later, he also attenuates desire through nebulous terms "something / perhaps precious / a little solace" (6-8) — the indefinite pronoun "something," the uncertain adverb "perhaps" and the diminutive qualifier "a little" all conveying ambiguity and mellowness. Yet against these abstractions emerge clear tactile descriptions: "nip her finger," "pecking sharply" (10-11), that betray Catullus' intimate desires with certainty. Indeed, while Catullus would have tried to ensure his "love" remains "ebbed" (9), his language, like desire itself, follows an inevitable tide of flow and retreat — just like the flitting pattern of lines on the page. Catullus struggles to maintain control, each endeavor at abstention recurrently undermined by lucid surges of fantasy he cannot suppress.

In fact, each bid to alienate himself from Lesbia paradoxically intensifies his desire. Initially, Catullus directly names Lesbia in his opening exclamation "Lesbia's sparrow! / Lesbia's plaything!" (1-2). Yet this is the closest he ever gets; he later relegates Lesbia to the third-person, but addresses the sparrow in the second-person "you." By closing the distance with the sparrow but widening it with Lesbia, the sparrow becomes an intermediary between Catullus and Lesbia. However, it paradoxically also becomes a hotbed for Catullus' fantasies that cannot be directed towards Lesbia herself. In his reverie, Catullus slips back into the first-person pronoun — "if I could play with you / her sparrow" (12-13) — conceding to desire and eroding his earlier undertaking to evade it. Lines also begin to blur between "you" and "her sparrow": the line trails off at "you," the line break and white space preserving "you" as the subject, before "her sparrow" is added on almost like an afterthought — or even an excuse. Catullus clarifies what "you" refers to, but it is precisely this act of clarification that suggests ambiguity with who he meant by "you." The carefulness of Catullus in self-correction thus betrays him having considered another "you" he wanted to play with — Lesbia. The distance the sparrow adds between Catullus and Lesbia belies a convenient pretext for Catullus to project his heightened sensual desires toward Lesbia — of "nip[ping]" and "pecking" — onto the sparrow's playful interactions with her instead.

The increasing distance culminates in Catullus' final allusion to "the girl" (16) — Atalanta, the virgin huntress forced to marry the man who won her in a race by beguiling her with golden apples (Howell and Howell 133). This mythological abstraction marks Catullus' furthest removal from Lesbia, who is no longer even referenced in the third person, but transmuted into allegory. Yet in this moment Catullus' desire emerges most explicitly. While earlier, his desire was characterized as abstract and intransitive, the myth allows Catullus to articulate his yearning with magnified carnality: "the miniature apple, / gold/undid / her girl's girdle" (17-19). Symbols from the myth are combined into overtly sexual imagery: the "apple," a symbol of seduction, unties the "girdle," a symbol of purity — the sensual overtones far exceed the sparrow's flirtatious "nip[ping]" and "pecking" in intensity. The intentional placement of the word "gold" next to "undid" gives it a dual function as adjective of "apple" to adverb of "undid," a syntactic move that accentuates the erotic charge of the action. Catullus' expression of desire also becomes almost forceful: he proclaims "I should be eased" (15), where "should" carries a double entendre of hypothetical possibility and of asserting entitlement — the latter the complete opposite of his feeble pining in "if I could play with you." By demanding his "ease[]" from the girl being "eased of her virginity" (16), Catullus abandons euphemism for plain declaration of lust. Thus even as he moves further from Lesbia into abstraction, this very distance paradoxically unleashes his most visceral, concrete and uninhibited expressions of desire.

Unrealized desire permeates Whigham's translation of "Catullus II," where what begins as a timid expression of infatuation evolves into an increasingly raw and desperate articulation of lust, leaving us with a man who, in striving to muffle his desire, only reveals its unrelenting power. Catullus demonstrates how poetic devices both restrain and release; perhaps to achieve true ease and self-reconciliation, what Catullus needs is to undo — "too long tied" (19) — not Lesbia's girdle, but his meticulously constructed bonds of emotional restraint. The girl, her sparrow, her plaything — and a desire for all of which, that perhaps should be released from the confines of hesitant line breaks, tentative diction, contrived grammar and abstract symbols.
