What is Hume's Problem of Induction? How Should We Respond to It?

Hume's problem of induction challenges our justification for believing the conclusion of an inductive argument on the basis of its premises. In this essay, I will first expound Hume's problem of induction, identifying a core assumption. Next, I argue that we should respond to Hume with Wright's concept of epistemic entitlement, thereby dissolving a false dilemma in his argument and rejecting inductive scepticism.

The Problem of Induction

Induction is defined as deriving conclusions about the unobserved by extrapolating from the observed (Millican, 2015). I adopt a broad interpretation of induction to encompass all synchronic and diachronic inferences (generalisations and predictions respectively), as both share the same lack of logical validity. Hume (1748) illustrates in his example:

P: Every time I have eaten bread it has nourished me.
Therefore,
C: Eating bread always nourishes me (generalisation). / The next time I eat bread it will nourish me (prediction).

This argument is different from a deductive one, which would contain premises that, if true, must lead to a single true conclusion; any other conclusion contradicts the premises. Contrarily, inductive arguments are not truth-preserving. The truth of P does not solely entail the truth of C. The negation of C, for instance, that bread will not nourish me, "is no less intelligible a proposition" as C, for one day bread may just all turn poisonous.

Now, consider C': The next time I eat bread, pigs will fly.

There is no contradiction between P and C', but C' obviously seems absurd: how do pigs even relate to my bread consumption? It is thus apparent that we do rely on some kind of justification for believing C is not a non-sequitur like C'.

Hume identifies that in such justification attempts, we invoke the Uniformity Principle (UP). Since "instances, of which we have had no experience, resemble those, of which we have had experience" (Hume, 1739), we can licence an inference from the unobserved to the observed.

Henceforth, Hume asserts the core problem of induction: there is no epistemic argument for the UP. The UP cannot be demonstratively justified with a priori reason as unobserved instances of non-uniformity (e.g. flying pigs) and observed instances of uniformity (e.g. non-flying pigs) are simultaneously conceivable. Neither can it be empirically justified with observations of the UP working previously, since that is inductive reasoning in itself, thus rule-circular and proving nothing.

Since the UP underpins all inductive reasoning, Hume concludes that inductive inferences cannot be justified. Ergo, the credibility of our empirical knowledge at the centre of the scientific method and our daily life is eroded.

Wright's Epistemic Entitlement

Despite such a radical conclusion, I propound Wright's concept of epistemic entitlement to restore our faith in inductive knowledge.

It is worth first noting Hume's implicit assumption that using empirical evidence to justify our inductive conclusions must be contingent upon having justified our belief in the inductive rule, UP; that having a first-order justification necessitates having a second-order one (Vogel, 2004). I accept his assumption that some link connects the two epistemic levels, as the reliability of the inductive method necessarily bears on the truth-conduciveness of inductive conclusions.

However, I argue that there is a false dilemma that either the UP is justified on the second-level, or the conclusion is not justified on the first-level. Merely a rational warrant—"that property of belief that serves as a reliable route to true belief and knowledge" (Graham & Pedersen, 2024)—is needed at the second-level to allow for first-order justification. Despite the absence of evidential warrant (justification), a non-evidential warrant (entitlement) is still possible. Epistemic entitlement thus supports our movement from premise to conclusion in inductive arguments.

Wright proposed epistemic entitlement to rationalise our beliefs in what he calls "cornerstones" of cognitive projects: foundations in "a region of knowledge" (Wright 2004, pp. 167–168). Wright presents why it is epistemically permissible to trust cornerstones for which "we cannot acquire any kind of evidence, either empirical or a priori" (Volpe, 2011):

"If a cognitive project is indispensable, or anyway sufficiently valuable to us—in particular, if its failure would at least be no worse than the costs of not executing it, and its success would be better—and if the attempt to vindicate (some of) its presuppositions would raise presuppositions of its own of no more secure an antecedent status, and so on ad infinitum, then we are entitled to—may help ourselves to, take for granted—the original presuppositions without specific evidence in their favour." (Wright 2004, p. 192)

The UP satisfies Wright's conditions for entitlement. It is a presupposition of all cognitive projects involving inquiry via induction — indispensable and valuable projects indeed. It is also a cornerstone due to infinitely regressive or viciously circular justification. Thus, despite lacking justification, we can trust the UP because (1) it is a cornerstone and (2) trusting it is a dominant strategy. Although we may not be justified in believing the UP, we are entitled to accept it—which means to be convinced of its truth and act as if it is true, having neither evidence in its favour nor grounds for doubt (Graham & Pedersen, 2024)—as that will maximise true beliefs and minimise false ones.

Critics may claim that Wright's dominant-strategy defence of the UP is pragmatic like Reichenbach's vindication of induction. I dispute this claim; Wright's entitlement does not, nor does it try to, pragmatically motivate the use of induction without establishing why we would be justified in believing inductive conclusions. It is epistemic in nature, being a warrant for accepting the UP on the second epistemic level, thereby enabling evidence to justify the conclusion of an inductive argument on the first epistemic level.

Conclusion

Hume's problem of induction is premised on the failure to justify the UP carrying over to the failure to justify the truth-conduciveness of an inductive belief. Nonetheless, I showed that since it is a cornerstone, we are epistemically entitled to it. Given that our entitlement to the UP can accord it with the same warrant for belief that justification hypothetically would have provided, I conclude that we should reject Humean scepticism as we do have grounds to believe the conclusion of an inductive argument based on its premises.
