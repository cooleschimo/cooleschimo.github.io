The Pieces Margaret Picked Up

Investigating the Psychological Ideal of Howards End

Based on the grounds of Jungian theory, Howards End presents the integration of the anima and the animus as a psychological ideal. The integration of the anima and the animus is epitomised by the novel's epigraph "Only connect...", as well as the Jungian process of individuation, which is the central concept of Jung's psychology. One of the requirements of Jung's psychological goal of individuation is the integration of the anima and the animus. The anima is the feminine archetype of a man's unconscious and similarly the animus the masculine in a woman's unconscious, (Jung CW 9ii, par. 27) and they sum up one's unconscious judgments (Meletinsky 46). When one integrates the archetypes, one is essentially keeping both masculine and feminine attributes of one's unconscious in harmony and balance. In tandem with this Jungian goal is Howards End's demonstration of the anima-animus integration as a psychological ideal, through the portrayal of characters with either an unintegrated anima or animus to complement a central character, Margaret, who has integrated both archetypes.

Henry Wilcox rejects his anima tremendously, resulting in what Jung claimed to be the "total loss of the anima" (CW 9i, par. 146). His characterisation advocates for the integration of the anima by illustrating the flaws of not doing so. A reference to Ulysses, "So the sailors of Ulysses voyaged past the sirens, having first stopped one another's ears with wool" (Forster 101), draws a parallel between the quintessential masculine role of Ulysses and Henry. Both are men who exhibit conventional masculinity by ignoring an important indication of their feminine side, emotions, or one could say, "the Sirens" of their anima.

Jung noted that complete rejection results in "stereotypy, fanatical one-sidedness, obstinacy, pedantry, or else resignation, weariness...and finally...ramollissement" (CW 9i, par. 147). When Henry refused Helen from his house, he reasoned that he could "not treat her as if nothing had happened" as that would render him "false to [his] position in society". (Forster 322). Clearly, Henry was stereotypical and pedantic, excessively clinging to social norms, thinking that "Helen's condition [would] depreciate the property" (Forster 322). Though the exact words were uttered by Margaret, she was indeed echoing Henry's beliefs that "Helen's condition" — such a taboo "condition" of being pregnant with Leonard's child — was intolerable in society, and thus also in his house. The word "condition" already connotes the stereotyping of Helen. To add on, Henry would, obstinately, not make an exception even for Margaret, his lover. These traits Jung listed, all of which apply to Henry, drove Margaret away from Henry. In Howards End, such a character without an anima receives an ending of weakening and defeat, perfectly tying in with what Jung calls "resignation, weariness...and finally...ramollissement". Forster's illustration of this particular character whose unintegrated anima led to his ultimate defeat evidently put such a psyche in an undesirable light, essentially being one of the cornerstones to presenting the ideal anima-animus integration.

Helen Schlegel then, is the accompanying counterexample of a woman possessed by the animus, instead of integrating it to strike a balance between her feminine and masculine tendencies. Helen is known to have a mind of her own, a sign of interdependent thinking and thus of an integrated animus. However, it is evident that it is not she who has her "mind", but that the "mind" has her (Martin 87). While the animus allows for "reflection, deliberation, and self-knowledge" in a woman (Jung CW 9ii, par. 33), it is also capable of turning her into a "dogmatist and high-handed pedagogue" (Jung CW 7, par. 336). For Helen, the latter clearly applies; she was animus-possessed, seeking to lay on others, as Margaret accurately phrased, her "perverted notion of philanthropy" (Forster 234). She only considers her own opinions as the absolute right, always seeking to impose them on others.

Bringing the Basts to Oniton saw her dogmatism at its height. She curtly disagreed with Margaret regarding their responsibility for the Basts: "No, indirectly. Via Mr Wilcox". Her sentences were short, exuding inexplicable confidence — but also dogmatism — as if it were a fact that Henry was mainly to blame. Without control over her anima, Helen's opinions, as Jung predicted, merely aim to "sever the relation between two human beings." (CW 9ii, par. 32) Undoubtedly, her visit to Oniton was full of animosity towards Henry and Margaret alike, but when she said "a great many scathing things about Henry" (Forster 237) to impose her opinions on Margaret and sever the couples' "relation", she yet only severed another "relation" between the sisters. Helen's high-handed opinions and dogmatic actions undoubtedly paint a negative picture of her character, thus discrediting psyches with an unintegrated animus, but complements the novel's advances towards presenting the ideal anima-animus integration.

Ultimately, Margaret Schlegel synthesises both the feminine and the masculine, taking on the essential role of validating such a psyche as an ideal. Margaret, having the epigraph "Only connect" as her "sermon" after all (Forster 195), drove the plot by harmonising her unconscious masculine and feminine qualities. Forster materialised this integration through her successful connection with other characters, because "the process of individuation must lead to more intense and broader collective relationships" (Jung CW 6, par. 758). Margaret forged connections with and between two internally disconnected people, Henry and Helen.

Firstly, when she integrated her animus, which Jung noted, requires the woman to "criticize and hold her opinions at a distance; not to repress them but to investigate their origins" (CW 7, par. 336), she connected with Henry. Margaret admitted that Henry was "deficient where she excelled" (Forster 107), but she knew to criticise her opinions: "How dare the Schlegels despise Wilcoxes, when it takes all sorts to make a world?" (Forster 107). In her query, her resolute attitude against the Schlegels' one-sided opinions of Wilcoxes emphasised her effort to integrate her animus and be rational, which explains why she could appreciate Henry for "excelling where she was deficient" (Forster 107) and connect with him. Secondly, besides integrating her animus, Margaret also embraced her anima qualities of "relationship and relatedness" (CW 9ii, par. 33). She was able to understand Helen's desire to stay at Howards End, as she related to Helen's attachment to the furniture, also understanding that Helen was not a sinner, but someone vulnerable who needed the comfort of a familiar environment and the accompaniment of family, to alleviate her internal turmoil.

Unfortunately, Henry was unable to connect with Helen like Margaret had, and vice versa. In such a situation, Margaret's psychic harmony was ideal not only because of its well-balanced nature that allowed her to connect with others, but also because it connected others. This internal harmony she sought to instill in Henry, to build "the rainbow bridge that should connect the prose...with the passion" (Forster 194), his masculine nature with his anima. She tried to have Henry connect with Helen: "You shall see the connection if it kills you, Henry!...Stupid, hypocritical, cruel...contemptible! Only say to yourself: 'What Helen has done, I've done'" (Forster 323). Margaret's use of harsh words, "stupid", "hypocritical", "cruel", "contemptible", and constant exclamations serve to trigger Henry's emotions of shame, guilt or regret, as he had always denied such emotions of vulnerability. Her lambasting certainly had an effect on Henry, albeit just creating a fissure in his emotionless cage of rigidity. It was this fissure, coupled with external events, that made him fall apart and become vulnerable, after which Margaret took him in to continue fostering a connection between him and Helen.

On Helen's side, Margaret had always explained rationally that Henry's qualities "which [she] so despise[s]" were what allowed England to progress from "Just savagery" (Forster 182). Margaret reminded Helen to accept, if not appreciate, but not despise Henry's different values: "eternal differences, planted by God...so that there may always be colour" (Forster 357). No matter how displeasing the "colour", or Henry, is to Helen, it is a colour in the colour spectrum after all, and would certainly contribute to making the world more colourful in its own way. Margaret wants Helen to learn to rationalise and see the other side of the story. Her anima-animus integration allowed her to connect others, ultimately bringing Helen and Henry together under one roof.

As Helen said to Margaret, "What a change — and all through you" (Forster 357). Clearly, she was the central character to capture the essence of Howards End — the epigraph "Only connect", to connect the disparate aspects of her own nature and to connect others, all of which portrays her positively and attests the ideal integration of the anima and the animus.
